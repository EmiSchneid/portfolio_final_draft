New to Photography?

Start with what you have! Thanks to smartphones cameras and their rapidly increasing quality, millions of us have access to an excellent camera. I became serious about photography while using an iPhone 5 because I had a strong desire to capture the magic of my beloved dog at the beach. I eventually bought a cropped-sensor Sony mirrorless camera and a couple of lenses and took thousands more photos of Skye. He passed away at nearly 17 years old in 2021. Some of those photos I took of him on my first iPhone remain among my favorites of the thousands I took of him.

“The camera is an instrument that teaches people how to see without a camera.” — Dorothea Lange

The most important thing I learned shooting on an iPhone was how to compose shots and that sometimes the best shot requires us to squat or kneel on the ground (hello, dog photography!). Tip: For landscape photos using a phone, tap the screen to focus on the far scenery so the whole image is in focus.

Tip: Be conscious of where you place the sun in your composition.

Tip: When you have leading lines, such as the railings, try different compositions to maintain the visual balance.

Tip: Keep the rule of thirds in mind when composing shots.

Tip: When the subject is in motion, compose the shot so there is more room ahead of the subject than behind them.

Tip: Always make sure the horizon is straight or edit them to be straight afterward.

Tip: White subjects can easily be overexposed and that is sometimes impossible to correct afterward so focus your exposure on your subject.

Tip: Be playful and look for ways to capture the personality of your subject indirectly, such as with shadows.

Tip: Tapping the phone on the shadows will increase exposure in that area of the photo.

Let's start with some definitions

No matter what camera you use, understanding some principles of photography can give you more shots you're happy with, and hopefully some you're thrilled with. A good place to start is knowing terms like exposure, composition, and resolution, which are impacted by aperture, shutter speed, ISO, and lens length or zoom. Check out the technical explanations below for a beginner-friendly rundown of these terms to get you started.

  • Exposure: The amount of light that hits the camera sensor (or film), which is largely controlled by how long the shutter is open and the size of the lens aperture. The visual results of exposure is how light or dark the photo turns out. On an iPhone, this can be adjusted by tapping the screen and then dragging up or down next to the box that appears.
  • Shutter: The shutter is a physical part of the camera that covers the sensor and opens and closes when a picture is taken. The speed at which the shutter opens is called shutter speed. Most phone cameras have automatic shutter speeds.
  • Aperture: A physical part of a lens that is The diameter of the opening of the aperture is adjustable and is labelled as numbers between 0.95 and 22, and written as f/0.95, f/7, f/22, etc. The smaller the number, the bigger the opening of the physical aperture. Smaller numbers/larger openings are referred to as "fast" lenses. The size of the aperture results in several visual changes in a photo: the depth of field, exposure (lightness/darkness). Depth of field is what makes the backgrounds on some portrait photos look blurry. The smaller the depth of field, the blurrier the background. Some people like this effect to make the subject stand out from the background. When phone cameras have a portrait mode, they are replicating a small depth of field digitally.
